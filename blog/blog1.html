How to perform a Breast Self Exam

Feb 18, 2017 / By Amelia Coomber

Knowing your normal is one of the most effective ways to detect something that’s not. At Norma, we are dedicated to providing you the tools and resources to take charge of your breast health so you ‘know your normal’.

Breasts are amazing and are always changing based off menstrual cycle, hormonal changes, and age. Some days they hurt, some days you feel lumps, and other days you might not notice anything.

Abnormalities come in lots of shapes and sizes, and it’s important to be prepared to detect something that doesn’t feel normal as soon as it arises. While finding something new can be scary, it is also important to know that most bumps or lumps are not cancerous. The most important task is to track it to see if it worsens, stays the same, or gets better.

At Norma, we are monthly breast self exam advocates because we believe it’s one of the most powerful ways to learn about your body and identify changes that don’t feel normal. Forming this lifelong habit and arming yourself with the knowledge to identify and address breast issues is a proactive approach to healthcare that we highly encourage.

Forty percent of diagnosed breast cancers are detected by women who feel a lump, so establishing a regular breast self-exam is very important. - Johns Hopkins University Breast Center

So how do you do a self breast exam?

1. The best time to do a breast self exam is 4-5 days after the last day of your menstrual period - this is when your hormonal cycle has the least impact on your breast tissue. For women who are postmenopausal, pick a day of the month that is easy for you to remember to do your exam.

2. Observe your breasts in the mirror with your hands on your hips. Look for any dimpling, redness, nipple discharge, or lumps and bumps. Turn left and right, then raise your arms and repeat your observations.

3. Now, either standing up or laying down, feel your left breast using your right hand. With the pads of your three fingers, move in small, quarter-size circles all the way around the breast, starting from outside (under the armpit) and moving into the center (sternum). Make sure to cover the entire breast, up towards the collarbone and under the armpit. Your lymphatic system is located under your armpit and is a very common place for abnormalities to develop.

4. Repeat this same motion on your right breast with your left hand.

5. While feeling for any lumps or bumps are important, also make sure to feel for an irregular orange peel-like texture around the breast and up towards the collar bone. This texture can often be caused by irritation of the lymphatic system and is a sign that something isn’t normal - we recommend you contact your doctor if you see this issue.

According to breastcancer.org, a breast cancer non-profit in the U.S., “Only changes that last beyond one full cycle, or seem to get bigger or more prominent in some way, need your doctor's attention.” You can track changes by writing down what you feel in a journal. In just a few months, Norma will be launching a mobile app that will track changes digitally and remind you to do your monthly breast self exam.

Taking charge of your breast health by performing monthly breast self exams is a huge step towards learning your normal so you can detect what’s not. Stay up-to-date with all that’s going on with Norma by signing up for our mailing list.
